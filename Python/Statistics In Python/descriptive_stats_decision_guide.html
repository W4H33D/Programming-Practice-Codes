Descriptive statistics decision guide — when to use mean, median, mode, range, and standard deviation

Measures of center

Center  Central tendency

Mean

Average value. Uses every number in the dataset. Fast and intuitive but pulled toward extremes.

Symmetric data No extreme outliers Avoid: skewed data

Center  Central tendency

Median

Middle value after sorting. Unaffected by outliers — only position matters, not magnitude.

Has outliers Skewed distributions Avoid: small datasets

Center  Central tendency

Mode

Most frequent value. Works on both numbers and text categories. Can be multiple values or none.

Categorical data Finding most common value Avoid: continuous data

Measures of spread

Spread  Dispersion

Range

Max minus min. Quick, simple, but uses only two values. Gives no information about middle values.

Quick overview Spotting extremes Avoid: detailed analysis

Spread  Dispersion

Standard deviation

Average distance of every point from the mean. Uses all data. Small = consistent. Large = variable.

Measuring consistency Comparing two datasets Avoid: categorical data
Watch out: Two datasets can share the same mean and range yet behave very differently. Always pair a center measure with a spread measure — a mean alone is incomplete.
Recommended combination: Use mean + standard deviation together for symmetric numerical data, and median + range when outliers are likely present. Add mode when categories matter.
